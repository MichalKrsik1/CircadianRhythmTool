Understanding Light Exposure & Sleep relationship

The circadian rhythm, our body's internal clock, influences our sleep patterns, energy levels, and overall health. Light exposure, both natural and artificial, plays a crucial role in regulating this rhythm. This guide is based on the research of neuroscientist Dr. Andrew Huberman and chronobiologist Dr. Samer Hattar.

Wake up later

Exposure to light during the "red zone" hours can delay your circadian rhythm, causing you to wake up later.

Wake up sooner

Light exposure during the "yellow zone" hours can advance your circadian rhythm, leading you to wake up earlier.

Maintain schedule

Consistent light exposure during the "green zone" hours will help stabilize your circadian rhythm and maintain your sleep schedule.

Dead zone

During the "grey zone" hours, your body is less responsive to light exposure.

Strategies

  • Engage with light during the hours you desire to be awake.
  • Limit light exposure during the hours you want to rest or prepare for sleep.

Helpful Hints

How much light is needed?

100k Lux, equivalent to about 5-15 minutes of sunlight exposure (depending on weather and location), is recommended. Artificial light is also effective but generally emits less lux.

How to manage light exposure?

It's not necessary to completely avoid light. During evenings, aim to reduce light to a level where you can just about manage to see. Your eyes can adjust to much lower light levels than you might expect.

Food and exercise during sleep hours

Hard exercise and heavy meals can have a similar effect to light on your circadian rhythm. Same rules apply for when to seek / avoid them as with light.
